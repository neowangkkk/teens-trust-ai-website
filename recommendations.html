Recommendations

Based on our findings, we propose actionable steps for the three groups best positioned to help teens navigate AI safely: schools, parents, and policymakers.

🏫

For Schools

  • 1

    Integrate AI literacy into curricula, including misinformation detection, ethical use, privacy basics, and AI limitations.

  • 2

    Provide clear, age-appropriate guidelines for academic use to reduce over-reliance and protect learning integrity.

  • 3

    Implement structured discussions about emotional boundaries with AI.

👨‍👩‍👧

For Parents

  • 1

    Encourage open dialogue about students' AI habits and discuss risk awareness, especially around privacy and emotional use.

  • 2

    Model responsible AI practices at home and align expectations with school guidelines.

🏛️

For Policymakers

  • 1

    Strengthen AI safety standards for youth, including data protection and transparency requirements.

  • 2

    Support teacher training to help educators guide students through safe and effective AI use.

  • 3

    Promote nationwide digital literacy initiatives that empower teens to critically evaluate AI outputs and manage risks.

Key Conclusions

AI is deeply integrated into student life, especially for academic tasks, and usage increases with grade level and among female students.

Risk awareness exists but remains low overall, especially for privacy, cybersecurity, and emotional risks.

Frequent users show significantly lower concern across all risk categories, suggesting that habituated use reduces perceived risk even when vulnerabilities remain.

A minority of teens use AI for emotional support, viewing it as empathetic or nonjudgmental, creating potential psychological risks.

The findings reveal a widening gap between actual risks and students' perceived risks, underscoring the need for targeted education.
