Student Voices

Behind the numbers are real conversations. Through 32 one-on-one interviews, students shared candid perspectives on how they use AI, what they share with it, and where they draw the line.

Data Sharing Attitudes

Students show a mix of casual openness and felt helplessness about their data.

“Like basically everything, like, you know, obviously not like my Social Security number, my like passport, not like those type of stuff, but like everything else, sure.”

Casual data sharing

“How much control do you feel you have over what personal data AI collects about you? 0%.”

Perceived helplessness

AI as Emotional Support

Some students turn to AI for therapy-like interactions, raising psychological safety concerns.

“Using ChatGPT for therapy or coping mechanisms and vent thoughts also works.”

AI as therapist

“People say don't do that but I don't see the harm in it.”

Dismissing concerns

“It's more empathetic and you don't bother anyone.”

Perceived empathy

“ChatGPT will pretty much align with whatever you say. It is less prominent but still there.”

Validation bias

Drawing Boundaries

Other students maintain clear limits on how they engage with AI.

“Never asks AI for emotional advice — only uses it for academic stuff.”

Academic-only use

“Friends would always be better for personal matters.”

Human preference

“AI is not my therapist.”

Clear boundary
